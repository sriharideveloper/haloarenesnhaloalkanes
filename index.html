Core Concepts & Fundamentals

BOILING POINT ORDER → R-I > R-Br > R-Cl > R-F (Hover to expand)

I Iodine
Highest BP
Most Van der Waals
Br Bromine
High BP
Cl Chlorine
Medium BP
F Fluorine
Lowest BP
Highest Electronegativity

DIPOLE MOMENT ORDER → R-Cl > R-F > R-Br > R-I (charge × distance, not just electronegativity!)

⬡
Classification
1°, 2° & 3° Haloalkanes
Primary (1°): Halogen on a carbon bonded to one other carbon.

Secondary (2°): Halogen on a carbon bonded to two other carbons.

Tertiary (3°): Halogen on a carbon bonded to three other carbons.
3° > 2° > 1° → SN1 reactivity (carbocation stability)
CH₃X > 1° > 2° > 3° → SN2 reactivity (steric factor)
🔗
Special Types
Allylic, Benzylic & Vinylic
Allylic: X on sp³ carbon next to C=C double bond (C=C–C–X)

Benzylic: X on sp³ carbon next to benzene ring (Ph–CH₂–X)

Vinylic: X directly on sp² carbon of a double bond (C=C–X)

  • Allylic & Benzylic → prefer SN1 (stable carbocation via resonance)
  • Vinylic → very unreactive to nucleophilic sub.
⚛️
Bond Nature
The C–X Bond
Halogens are more electronegative than carbon → C–X bond is polar

Carbon gets δ+ charge; Halogen gets δ− charge

Bond Strength: C–F > C–Cl > C–Br > C–I
Bond Length: C–F < C–Cl < C–Br < C–I

⚠️ Dipole Moment Trap: CH₃Cl > CH₃F despite F being more electronegative! Bond length wins here.
🔬
Hybridization
Haloalkane vs Haloarene
Haloalkane: X attached to sp³ hybridized carbon → reactive, undergoes SN1/SN2

Haloarene: X attached to sp² hybridized carbon of aromatic ring → very unreactive

  • sp² carbon is more electronegative → holds electrons tighter
  • Lone pairs of X participate in resonance → C–X gets partial double bond character
  • Phenyl cation is very unstable → SN1 ruled out
💧
Physical Properties
Boiling Point & Solubility
Boiling Point Rules:
  • R–I > R–Br > R–Cl > R–F → More massive = stronger Van der Waals
  • Branching ↑ → BP ↓ (more spherical = less surface area = weaker VdW)
  • Chain length ↑ → BP ↑ (more surface area)
  • Alkyl halides > Alkanes (dipole-dipole > Van der Waals only)

Solubility: Haloalkanes are insoluble in water — they can't form H-bonds with water, and energy released by new interactions is insufficient to break H-bonds in water.
Para-dichlorobenzene: highest MP of isomers (most symmetrical → best crystal packing)
🎯
Directing Effects
Halogen on Benzene Ring
Halogens on benzene show dual behavior:

–I effect (inductive): Withdraws electrons → deactivates ring overall

+R effect (resonance): Donates lone pairs into ring → increases electron density specifically at ortho and para positions

∴ Halogens are RING DEACTIVATORS but ORTHO/PARA DIRECTORS for EAS
Major product is usually para (less steric hindrance than ortho).
🔀
Nucleophiles
Ambident Nucleophiles
Nucleophile: Electron-rich species (Lewis base) that attacks a positive center. Examples: OH⁻, Cl⁻, H₂O, NH₃

Ambident Nucleophile: Has two possible attack sites

CN⁻ (Cyanide):
• KCN (ionic) → attacks via Carbon → Cyanide (R–CN)
• AgCN (covalent) → attacks via Nitrogen → Isocyanide (R–NC)

NO₂⁻ (Nitrite):
• KNO₂ (ionic) → attacks via Oxygen → Alkyl nitrite (R–O–N=O)
• AgNO₂ (covalent) → attacks via Nitrogen → Nitroalkane (R–NO₂)
📏
Nomenclature
IUPAC Naming Rules
  1. Find longest chain containing the halogen
  2. Number from the end giving halogen the lowest locant
  3. Halogens are named as prefixes: fluoro-, chloro-, bromo-, iodo-
  4. List substituents in alphabetical order
  5. For haloarenes: use prefix system with position numbers
Example: 2-Bromo-3-chlorobutane (bromo before chloro = alphabetical)

SN1 vs SN2 — The Battle Table

FEATURE
⚡ SN1 — Unimolecular
🎯 SN2 — Bimolecular
STEPS
2️⃣Two steps — forms carbocation intermediate THEN nucleophile attacks
1️⃣One step — concerted mechanism; bond breaking & forming happen SIMULTANEOUSLY
RATE LAW
Rate = k[substrate] only — independent of nucleophile concentration
Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile] — depends on BOTH
REACTIVITY ORDER
3° > 2° > 1° > CH₃X (more substituted = more stable carbocation)
CH₃X > 1° > 2° > 3° (less substituted = less steric hindrance for backside attack)
STEREOCHEMISTRY
🔄 Racemization → 50:50 mixture of enantiomers. Planar carbocation attacked from both sides equally
🔃 Inversion of Configuration (Walden Inversion). Nucleophile attacks from BACK, flips the molecule like an umbrella in wind
INTERMEDIATE
Carbocation (sp² hybridized, planar, stable with 3° & resonance)
Transition state only — pentacoordinate carbon (trigonal bipyramidal)
SOLVENT
Polar PROTIC solvents (water, alcohols) — solvate and stabilize carbocation + leaving group via H-bonding
Polar APROTIC solvents (acetone, DMSO, DMF) — don't solvate nucleophile, keeps it "naked" and reactive
SUBSTRATE PREFERENCE
Tertiary haloalkanes, Allylic/Benzylic halides (resonance-stabilized carbocations)
Methyl and primary haloalkanes (unhindered)
NUCLEOPHILE STRENGTH
Weak nucleophiles work fine (H₂O, ROH) since it's a 2-step process
Strong nucleophiles required (OH⁻, CN⁻, I⁻) — must attack in one step

β-Elimination & Saytzeff's Rule

Elimination Mechanism
β-Elimination (Dehydrohalogenation)
When a haloalkane is heated with alcoholic KOH:

• H is removed from the β-carbon (adjacent to C bearing X)
• X is removed from the α-carbon
• A double bond (alkene) is formed

Aqueous KOH → provides OH⁻ (good nucleophile) → substitution (SN2)

Alcoholic KOH → provides RO⁻ (strong base) → elimination
Regiochemistry
Saytzeff's Rule
The major product of dehydrohalogenation is the more substituted alkene (more alkyl groups on double-bonded carbons) because it is more stable (more hyperconjugation).

Markovnikov's Rule: When HX adds to asymmetrical alkene, X⁻ goes to carbon with fewer H atoms

Anti-Markovnikov (Peroxide Effect): Only with HBr + peroxides → Br goes to carbon with more H atoms (free radical mechanism)

Stereochemistry — All Definitions

Stereochemistry

Chiral Carbon

A carbon bonded to four different groups (A, B, C, D). Also called an asymmetric carbon.

Stereochemistry

Enantiomers

Non-superimposable mirror images. Rotate plane-polarized light equally but in opposite directions.

Stereochemistry

Racemic Mixture

Equal mixture of two enantiomers → zero optical activity. Rotations cancel out perfectly. Formed by SN1 mechanism.

Stereochemistry

Walden Inversion

In SN2: nucleophile attacks from backside → configuration flips like an umbrella in wind → inverted product.

Optical Activity

Plane-Polarized Light

Ordinary light → vibrates in ALL planes

Polarizer → allows only ONE plane

Chiral molecule → rotates that plane

Detector → measures angle of rotation

Chiral molecules rotate the plane of polarized light. Dextrorotatory (+) = clockwise; Laevorotatory (−) = anticlockwise.

Key Fact

(±) Butan-2-ol

Optically
Inactive ✓

The (±) symbol denotes a racemic mixture. The optical rotation of (+) and (−) isomers cancel perfectly → net rotation = 0.

Name Reactions — Full Arsenal

Finkelstein Reaction
Halogen Exchange · SN2
R–Cl (or R–Br) + NaI → R–I + NaCl
Solvent: Dry Acetone
Driving force: NaCl/NaBr is insoluble in acetone → precipitates out → equilibrium shifts forward
Think: FinkelstEIn = Iodine (the 'I' in Einstein is the hint!)
Swarts Reaction
Fluorination · Halogen Exchange
R–Cl + AgF (or Hg₂F₂) → R–F + AgCl
Best method for making alkyl fluorides.
Can't use HF directly — too dangerous.
Swarts = Silver/Fluoride. "Swart" sounds like "swap" — swapping for Fluorine.
Wurtz Reaction
Carbon Chain Coupling · Free Radical
2 R–X + 2 Na → R–R + 2 NaX
Solvent: Dry Ether
Product: Symmetrical alkane (DOUBLE the carbons!)
Only works well with primary alkyl halides.
Wurtz = "Worth it" — doubles your carbon chain. 2 halides → 1 big alkane.
Fittig Reaction
Aryl Coupling · Sodium Metal
2 Ar–X + 2 Na → Ar–Ar + 2 NaX
Solvent: Dry Ether
Product: Biphenyl/Diphenyl (two benzene rings joined)
Fittig = 2 rings Fitting together. Aryl + Aryl = Biphenyl.
Wurtz–Fittig Reaction
Mixed Coupling · Alkylbenzene Synthesis
Ar–X + R–X + 2 Na → Ar–R + 2 NaX
Solvent: Dry Ether
Product: Alkylbenzene (arene)
One aryl halide + one alkyl halide
Wurtz-Fittig = Best of both worlds. Alkyl meets Aryl, sodium joins them.
Sandmeyer's Reaction
Haloarene Synthesis from Diazonium Salt
C₆H₅N₂⁺Cl⁻ + Cu₂Cl₂/HCl → C₆H₅Cl + N₂
C₆H₅N₂⁺Cl⁻ + Cu₂Br₂/HBr → C₆H₅Br + N₂
Catalyst: Cuprous halide (Cu₂X₂)
Starting material: Benzene diazonium chloride
Sandmeyer = Sand + Meyer. Cuprous (Cu) catalyst is KEY. Diazonium → Haloarene.
Friedel–Crafts Reactions
Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)
ALKYLATION: Ar–H + R–Cl → Ar–R + HCl [AlCl₃, anhydrous]
ACYLATION: Ar–H + R–COCl → Ar–COR + HCl [AlCl₃, anhydrous]
Catalyst: Anhydrous AlCl₃ (Lewis acid)
Note: Haloarenes undergo F-C slowly (ring deactivated by –I effect)
Friedel-Crafts: "Friend adding stuff to the ring." AlCl₃ is the MANDATORY catalyst.
Grignard Reagent (RMgX)
Organometallic Reagent Synthesis
R–X + Mg → R–Mg–X (in dry ether)
Example: CH₃Br + Mg → CH₃MgBr (Methylmagnesium bromide)
⚠️ STRICTLY ANHYDROUS CONDITIONS REQUIRED
RMgX + H₂O → R–H + Mg(OH)X (destroyed!)
Grignard = "Guaranteed Ruined If Moisture Near." Water destroys it instantly!

⚡ Last-Minute Cheat Sheet

🔵 Boiling Point Rules
Halogen size ↑ → BP ↑ (VdW forces)
Branching ↑ → BP ↓ (less surface area)
Chain length ↑ → BP ↑
R–I > R–Br > R–Cl > R–F order
Alkyl halides > Alkanes (polar > non-polar)
🟡 SN1 Bullets
2 steps → carbocation intermediate
3° > 2° > 1° reactivity
Racemization → 50:50 enantiomers
Polar protic solvent (water, alcohol)
Weak Nu OK → H₂O, ROH work fine
🎯 SN2 Bullets
1 step concerted → transition state
CH₃X > 1° > 2° > 3° reactivity
Inversion → Walden inversion
Polar aprotic solvent (DMSO, acetone)
Strong Nu needed → OH⁻, CN⁻, I⁻
🟠 Name Reactions Quick-Fire
Finkelstein → NaI/acetone → R–I
Swarts → AgF → R–F (fluorination)
Wurtz → 2RX + 2Na → R–R (alkane)
Fittig → 2ArX + 2Na → Ar–Ar
Wurtz-Fittig → ArX + RX + 2Na → Ar–R
Sandmeyer → Diazonium + Cu₂X₂ → ArX
🟢 Haloarene 3 Reasons
Resonance → C–X partial double bond
sp² carbon → more electronegative
Phenyl cation → highly unstable
BUT ortho/para director +R effect wins position
Nitro at o/p → activates Nu. sub.

Chloroform dark bottle → prevents phosgene
Grignard anhydrous → water destroys it
KCN → cyanide C attacks (ionic KCN)
AgCN → isocyanide N attacks (covalent)
H₃PO₄ with KI not H₂SO₄ (oxidizes HI)
Iodination + HNO₃ removes HI (reducing agent)
